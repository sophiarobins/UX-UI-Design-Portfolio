01 — UX Audit Findings

Nine problems.
One root cause.

HoneyBook is built for the photographer's operations dashboard — and almost every UX problem flows from the same design assumption: that the client's experience is secondary. These aren't edge cases. They're the daily reality of every photographer who uses it.

Critical

No enforced process order

Clients can pay before signing a contract. They can skip questionnaires entirely. HoneyBook puts tasks in front of clients with no guardrails — the business owner has to manually manage sequencing. This is a fundamental IA failure for any service business with legal and financial dependencies.

Impact: Photographers risk taking payment without a signed contract — a legal and financial exposure HoneyBook's UI actively enables.
Critical

Emails land in spam

HoneyBook sends automated emails from shared infrastructure. Because many users send bulk communications through the same sending domain, the domain reputation suffers. Couples routinely miss contracts, payment requests, and appointment reminders because HoneyBook's emails go directly to their spam folder.

Impact: The entire client communication model — the product's core value — becomes unreliable. Photographers end up calling or texting to chase their own tool's emails.
Critical

Zero brand customisation

The HoneyBook client-facing interface is HoneyBook's brand, not yours. You can add a logo and change an accent colour. Everything else — the fonts, the layout, the email templates, the portal structure — is locked. For a premium wedding photographer, handing clients a generic CRM portal is a brand contradiction.

Impact: The tool actively undermines the premium positioning photographers spend thousands building. Clients notice the inconsistency even when they can't name it.
Major

Months to set up properly

Templates, workflows, automations, pipelines, and smart fields all require extensive configuration before HoneyBook delivers its value. The onboarding documentation exists, but the learning curve is steep enough that most photographers use a fraction of what they're paying for — often for months.

Impact: Expensive from day one, useful much later. The ROI window is long, and many photographers abandon the tool before reaching it.
Major

Too many steps for clients

The client-facing portal requires couples to navigate multiple separate pages — contracts, invoices, questionnaires, scheduling — each accessed via separate links or tabs with no unified journey. There's no "here's where you are" orientation. Couples frequently call photographers to ask what they're supposed to do next.

Impact: The photographer has to onboard their clients into a third-party tool — adding friction to a relationship that should feel effortless from the couple's side.
Major

Integrations constantly break

HoneyBook's third-party integrations — calendar sync, payment processors, email clients — are persistently unreliable. Integration failures require manual intervention and generate no meaningful error messaging. Photographers discover broken integrations when a client's payment fails, not before.

Impact: Trust breaks at the worst possible moment — during financial transactions. A broken Stripe integration during retainer collection is an unacceptable failure mode.
Friction

Adapts to HoneyBook, not you

HoneyBook's pipeline and workflow system is built around generic freelance project stages. Wedding photography has a specific, non-negotiable sequence — booking, contract, retainer, questionnaire, planning call, wedding day, gallery delivery. Forcing that into HoneyBook's generic "pipeline" requires awkward workarounds that still don't feel right.

Impact: Every workflow decision is a compromise. The tool is never quite right for the job it's being asked to do.
Friction

$30–90/month for partial fit

HoneyBook's pricing tiers lock key features — automation, multiple pipelines, scheduling — behind higher plans. Small wedding businesses often end up on the expensive tier to access what they actually need, paying a premium for a tool that still requires workarounds. For a solo photographer, that cost compounds quickly across a year.

Impact: Paying $1,080/year for a tool you've adapted around — rather than a tool that adapts to you — is a poor return on investment.
Friction

No sense of client journey

From the client's perspective, there is no visible progression through the wedding photography process. HoneyBook shows tasks and documents, but nothing that says "you're here, this is next, this is what's coming." For couples who are often anxious about the details of their wedding planning, this absence creates unnecessary uncertainty.

Impact: Clients feel managed, not taken care of. The emotional job of a client portal — creating confidence and excitement — is entirely unaddressed.
"HoneyBook is a CRM that photographers use. What I needed was a client portal that couples would love. Those are two completely different products." — The design insight that started the Chasing Robins Client Portal
02 — Design Process

From audit
to redesign.

The audit identified nine specific failures. Rather than stopping at critique, I mapped the direct design response to each one — showing what a client-first CRM would actually look like. The Chasing Robins Client Portal is the shipped version of these ideas.

Before: HoneyBook's client portal — flat task list, no journey context, generic interface, no process enforcement.
After: A sequenced emotional journey — countdown timer, journey timeline, enforced workflow, fully branded client portal.
Key decision 01

Replaced HoneyBook's flat task list with a sequenced emotional journey — couples always know where they are and what's next.

Key decision 02

Payment only appears after contract is signed — enforced sequencing removes a legal and financial risk HoneyBook's UI actively enables.

Key decision 03

Welcome message with onboarding video walks couples through the portal on first login — replacing the anxiety of a blank unfamiliar interface.

03 — What I'd Fix in HoneyBook

If I were on
their design team.

Building my own solution doesn't mean HoneyBook is unsalvageable. These are the four highest-impact changes I'd prioritise if I were a designer at HoneyBook — the interventions that would close the gap between admin tool and client experience.

Priority 01 — Critical

Enforce a client journey

Replace the flat task list with a visual, sequential journey that gates each step behind the previous one. Couples should never see "Pay retainer" before they've signed. This is a single IA change with enormous downstream impact on both the client experience and the photographer's peace of mind.

Redesign the client home screen around a visual progress tracker
Add step-locking logic: payment unlocks post-signature only
Show "what's next" at every completed step
Priority 02 — Critical

Fix the email infrastructure

Move to a dedicated sending domain per account, or give photographers the ability to connect their own sending domain via DKIM/SPF records. A CRM whose emails land in spam is a CRM that doesn't work. This is a trust-breaking failure that no UI redesign can paper over.

Allow custom sending domain connection in account settings
Add email deliverability health indicator in the dashboard
Default to in-portal notifications with email as fallback
Priority 03 — Major

Deep brand customisation

Open up full font selection (at minimum, serif vs sans-serif pairing), background colour choices for the client portal, and the ability to suppress HoneyBook branding entirely on paid plans. The client-facing portal should be an extension of the photographer's brand — not a white-labelled generic SaaS interface.

Font pairing selector: display font + body font
Full colour system: background, surface, accent, text
White-label mode: remove all HoneyBook branding on Pro tier
Priority 04 — Major

Industry-specific templates

HoneyBook serves wedding photographers, event planners, interior designers, and brand designers — all with different workflows. Rather than forcing everyone into a generic pipeline model, offer industry-specific setup paths that pre-configure the pipeline, templates, and automation to match how that business actually works.

Onboarding: "What kind of business are you?" → tailored setup
Pre-built pipeline for wedding photography: 7-stage sequence
Industry-specific questionnaire and contract templates included

06 — Your Figma Work Plan

What to build
in Figma.

You don't need to redesign all of HoneyBook. You need two or three screens that show targeted, reasoned improvements. Here's exactly what to build and how to present it.

Screen 1 — Client portal home

  • → Add a visual journey tracker at the top showing all 7 stages
  • → Show a countdown to the wedding date as the emotional anchor
  • → Lock inaccessible steps visually — greyed out with a padlock
  • → Add a "what's next" prompt card highlighting the active task
  • → Show the photographer's face and a personal welcome note
  • → Annotate: "This replaces the flat task list with an emotional journey"

Screen 2 — Contract + payment flow

  • → Show contract step with a clear "Sign to unlock payment" message
  • → Payment CTA is greyed out and non-interactive before signing
  • → After signature: payment step animates to active state
  • → Progress indicator shows "Step 2 of 2 — You're almost there"
  • → Annotate: "Enforced sequencing removes a legal and trust risk"
  • → Prototype the unlock interaction in Figma — make it feel satisfying

Screen 3 — Branding system

  • → Show a "Brand settings" page with full font + colour control
  • → Live preview panel: changes update the portal preview in real time
  • → Offer 3 preset brand palettes + custom option
  • → Font pairing selector: serif display + sans body (Google Fonts)
  • → White-label toggle: "Remove HoneyBook branding" on Pro plan
  • → Annotate: "Brand consistency is the feature premium clients are paying for"

How to present in your case study

  • → Lead with a side-by-side: current HoneyBook screenshot vs your redesign
  • → Annotate every design decision with a "why" — not just "what"
  • → Link the Figma redesign directly to the audit findings above it
  • → Include the Chasing Robins portal as the "live version" of these ideas
  • → End with: "I didn't just critique HoneyBook — I shipped the alternative"
  • → Keep the prototype to 3 screens max — depth over breadth
